SPECPOL

The Special Political and Decolonization Committee is the fourth committee of the UN General Assembly. It deals with a variety of subjects which include those related to decolonization, Palestinian refugees and human rights, peacekeeping, mine action, outer space, public information, atomic radiation and University for Peace. The SPECPOL is charged with the goal of discussing and taking action on some of the most pressing issues in our rapidly changing global society.

Agenda 1: The status of the Crimean Peninsula.

For centuries, the Crimean Peninsula, which occupies a strategically important location in the Black Sea and has arable land, has been fought over by various outside forces. After the October Revolution ended the Russian Empire in 1917, Crimea found itself to be a sovereign state. However, this didn’t last long as it was quickly dragged into the Russian Civil War, where it became a stronghold for the White Army. The Euromaidan protests have frequently been portrayed as a battle between the pro-European West and the pro-Russian East, a legacy of Ukraine’s own history of Russian domination. This could be something of an oversimplification, but it is an idea that resonates with many, both abroad and within Ukraine.


Agenda 2: Territorial claims on Antarctica.

Sovereignty in Antarctica is important to Australia, even though its claim is not supported by most of the other Antarctic Treaty countries. The Treaty puts aside disputes over territorial claims by providing that no activities that take place while the Treaty is in force can be used to assert, support, deny or make a claim. Having a strong international presence in Antarctica minimizes the risk of external threats to Australia from that direction. If there were no Treaty, could the number of threats be controlled?
