GENERAL ASSEMBLY 1971

The United Nations General Assembly is one of the six principal organs of the United Nations and the only one in which all member nations have equal representation. The General Assembly meets under its president or Secretary-General in regular annual sessions. The Assembly may make recommendations on any matters within the scope of the UN, except matters of peace and security under Security Council consideration.

The Historical Committee is a special committee that aims at exploring the past sittings of the UN General Assembly with relevance to the agenda. This is done with the intent to recreate as much as possible the conditions of the original debate in real time. Owing to the historical nature of this committee, delegates must refrain from using information or examples from beyond 1971.

1971: The Conflict in East Pakistan

The origins of the 1971 Indo-Pak war can be traced back to the long standing dispute between the two countries over a multitude of factors, the most prominent conflict of which being the Bangladesh Liberation War. The Indo-Pak relations deteriorated when this civil war erupted in Pakistan, pitting the West Pakistan army against East Pakistanis demanding greater autonomy. Political instability and internal dissent continued to plague the country and all attempts to suppress dissent were to no avail.

A series of major engagements in the Western Sector, such as the Battle of Longewala, which was fought between assaulting Pakistani forces and Indian defenders, were a result of this war and as the tension between the two countries continued to escalate, with air operations and naval hostilities beginning to gain ground, the need for international intervention was imperative.
