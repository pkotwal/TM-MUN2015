UNODC

The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) is a global leader in the fight against illicit drugs and international crime. Established in 1997 through a merger between the United Nations Drug Control Programme and the Centre for International Crime Prevention, UNODC is mandated to assist Member States in their struggle against illicit drugs, crime and terrorism. In the Millennium Declaration, Member States also resolved to intensify efforts to fight transnational crime in all its dimensions, to redouble the efforts to implement the commitment to counter the world drug problem and to take concerted action against international terrorism.

Agenda 1: Illegal Exploitation of Wild Flora and Fauna in Asia.

Each year, hundreds of species of plants and animals are caught or harvested from the wild and traded illegally. From the depths of the oceans to unchartered territories, there is very little of the environment that has been spared. Nearly all species of flora and fauna have been drawn into this vast trade, and have been exploited ruthlessly for a multitude of purposes. Whether it is for medicinal purposes, souvenirs, clothing or simply to satisfy the palate of demanding customers, these trading activities all have one thing in common: a direct and devastating effect on our plant and animal populations, notably the ones in danger of extinction.

Although humans and animals are entities that appear to be vastly different, our destinies are closely intertwined. With the meter spinning out of control and human population increasing at an exponential rate, this is a matter that needs to be addressed, to ensure the survival of not only the flora and fauna, but also human life.


Agenda 2: Trade of Illicit Narcotics in South America and Mexico.

The illegal drug trade is a global black market dedicated to the cultivation, distribution and sale of drugs that are subject to drug prohibition laws. Although there are numerous regions all over the world that engage in this illicit trade, Mexico and South America have risen to prominence, and the drug trade in this region continues to gain ground.

Given its geographic location, the region has long been used as a staging and transhipment point for narcotics and contraband between Latin America and the US. Although Mexican drug cartels, or drug trafficking organizations, have existed for several decades, their influence has drastically increased of late. There are many factors contributing to the escalating violence in South America and Mexico and the fighting between drug cartels in this region has steadily worsened with time, with the result that by the end of the year 2012, the official death toll of the Mexican Drug War was 60,000.

Given recent events, illegal drug trade is proving to be remarkably resilient and without suitable intervention, will lead to severe consequences.
